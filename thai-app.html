Foundation

Before You Speak —
Understand the Culture

Thai language is deeply tied to respect, spirituality, and social harmony. Learn this first.

🦶

Feet & Pointing

  • Feet are considered the lowest, most spiritually impure part of the body
  • Never point your feet at people, Buddha images, or sacred objects
  • Don't use your feet to move objects or gesture — it's deeply offensive
  • When sitting, tuck feet away or sit cross-legged
👗

Dress Modestly

  • Thailand is a conservative Buddhist country — dress respectfully, especially at temples
  • Shoulders and knees must be covered when entering temples (wats)
  • Avoid very short shorts, crop tops, or revealing clothing in public religious spaces
  • Many temples provide sarongs at the entrance if needed
🙏

The Wai (ไหว้)

  • The wai is a greeting of respect — palms pressed together, slight bow
  • The higher your hands and the deeper your bow, the more respect you show
  • Younger/lower-status people initiate the wai first
  • Do NOT wai children, service workers, or in casual settings — it can feel awkward
  • As a foreigner, a small wai back is always appreciated and respectful
🙏 ไหว้ — a gesture that says everything without a word
👑

The Royal Family

  • The monarchy is deeply revered — criticism is illegal under lèse-majesté laws
  • Always stand for the royal anthem (played before movies, at public events)
  • Treat images, currency, and references to the royal family with respect
🗣️

Male & Female Speech Endings

  • Thai has gendered polite particles added to the end of sentences
  • Men say: ครับ (khrap) — used to be polite, affirm, or say yes
  • Women say: ค่ะ (kha) — falling tone for statements/affirmations
  • Women say: คะ (kha) — rising tone for questions
  • These are NOT optional — skipping them sounds rude or abrupt
"Thank you" → Men: ขอบคุณครับ  |  Women: ขอบคุณค่ะ

🌅 Good Afternoon?

No one really says "good afternoon" (สวัสดี ตอน บ่าย). Thais just say สวัสดี (Sawatdee) any time of day. Keep it simple.

♀️ Women — The Changing Particle

The polite particle kha changes tone depending on the sentence:

SituationParticleExample
Statementsค่ะ (kha) — fallingSabai dee kha ✓
Questionsคะ (kha) — risingSabai dee mai kha? ✓

Men always use ครับ (khrap) — one form for everything.

🌀 ไม่เป็นไร (Mai pen rai) — More Than "You're Welcome"

Literally "it's not anything" — covers you're welcome, no problem, it's okay, and don't worry.

🙏 Who Greets First?

The younger person initiates. If you see an older co-worker or friend, say hello first. Showing this cultural knowledge goes a long way.

🗣️ ขอโทษ (Khaaw thooht) — Versatile Phrase

Both "excuse me" and "I'm sorry". Literally "I take the blame." You'll use this a lot your first weeks.

🧑‍🤝‍🧑 Gendered pronouns for "I"

Thai has two words for "I" depending on the speaker's gender. Men use ผม (phom), women use ฉัน (chan). Both are polite and natural — use whichever matches you.

🙏 ขอโทษ (Khaaw thooht) — Excuse me & Sorry

This phrase covers both "excuse me" (to get attention or pass someone) and "I'm sorry". The literal meaning is "I take the blame." You'll use it constantly in your first weeks.

❓ ...ไหม (...mai) — Turning statements into questions

Add ไหม (mai) to the end of any statement to make it a yes/no question — like a rising inflection in English. Sabai dee = I'm well. Sabai dee mai? = Are you well?

💬 ...ล่ะ (...la) — "And you?"

...la? is a casual follow-up particle meaning "and you?" or "what about you?". After someone asks how you are and you reply, tack on khun la? to bounce it back politely.

💡 How to use numbers with baht

Say the number first, then baht — just like in English: sip baht = 10 baht, roi baht = 100 baht.

100 บาทroi baht100 baht (~$3)

🔢 Compound numbers — how to count past 10

Thai numbers follow a simple pattern: tens + ones. Sip (10) + neung (1) = sip-et (11). But there are two quirks worth knowing:

NumberRomanizationNote
11sip-et⚠️ 1 at the end becomes et, not neung
12sip-songRegular pattern
13sip-samRegular pattern
15sip-haRegular pattern
19sip-kaoRegular pattern
20yee-sip⚠️ 20 is yee-sip, NOT song-sip

⚠️ The two big rules to remember

Rule 1: When 1 appears at the end of a number (11, 21, 31...) it becomes et — not neung.
sip-et ✓    sip-neung ✗

Rule 2: 20 is always yee-sip — never song-sip.
